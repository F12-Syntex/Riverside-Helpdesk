THE RIVERSIDE PRACTICE
PPE POLICY
Introduction
The requirements of the PPE at Work Regulations 1992 apply to all employers, organisations and businesses. All staff have a responsibility to support a culture of promoting safe and best practice within the workplace and their job roles for their own and public safety.

The underlying rule of the Regulations is that Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) is to be supplied and used in the workplace wherever there are risks to health and safety that cannot be adequately controlled in other ways.

Essentially, this means that PPE should be used as a last resort.

Under the COSHH Regulations the list below covers the hierarchy of control which should be applied when assessing risk:

Elimination;
Substitution;
Segregation;
Ventilation;
Local exhaust variation;
PPE.

PPE in the GP Setting

It would be fair to assume there are a greater number of potential hazards for healthcare workers than those from other workplaces.

Predominantly, the greatest risk for healthcare workers in General Practice is the risk of infection from patients visiting the Practice.

Because of the practical nature of the job of treating people who are ill, the first five options in the list of control in the previous section are generally not practical, or sometimes even possible. This leaves PPE as the required option of control.

With regard to infection control, the purpose of PPE could be defined as 'to minimise the risk of transmission of infectious agents between patients, healthcare workers, and others within the General Practice setting'.

Employees must be aware that any breach of safety will place staff, patients and visitors at risk, and should follow the guidance required for such situations. The use of PPE minimises risks of injury or infection.

General Rules on PPE Standards

It is to be remembered that where PPE is used it must always be of the correct type, in sufficient quantity and fit-for-purpose. PPE used at the Practice must be:

Selected appropriately, judging the nature of the task undertaken and the potential risk involved;
Carry either an "EC" or a "BS" mark of quality;
In efficient working order and good repair;
Compatible with other complementary forms of PPE;
Clearly labelled and stored in a clean, dry and easily accessible area;
Supplied complete with appropriate usage instructions;
Only given to those persons trained in its correct operation;
Disposed of appropriately through the proper channels and in conjunction with infection control principles.

Where religious beliefs conflict with PPE requirement, this should be disclosed to the Practice and an assessment of alternatives will be considered.

'Bare Below the Elbow' : Infection Prevention and Control

All staff working in a direct clinical role (whether they wear a uniform or not) MUST apply the 'bare below the elbow' values whilst delivering care.

All wrist and hand jewellery must be removed (one plain ring with no stones or deep engraving is allowed)
No false nails, nail varnish or nail decorations
No long sleeves below the elbow

When carrying out clinical examinations or procedures in the practice, long garments (e.g. cardigans) must be removed. Where long sleeved garments cannot be removed (i.e. blouses and shirts), the sleeves must be rolled up to above elbow height, and for them to remain up on their own for the duration of the procedure.

Types of PPE and Example Uses

There are many types of PPE available to protect different areas of the body, but specifically the main areas which may be exposed to the risk of infection when meeting and consulting with patients and which require protection are the following:
Hands;
Body;
Eyes;
Mouth;
Face;
Respiratory;
Feet.

The PPE used to protect the above areas comprises the following groups, including examples of their usage. This list is not exhaustive:

Gloves / gauntlets / sleeving
Protect user from the risk of cuts and abrasions;
Prevent infection from passing from patient to healthcare worker, or vice-versa;
Stop irritation from the handling of drugs, chemicals or other substances;
Keep user's hands and arms clean.

Aprons / gowns / liquid repellent clothing
Protect user from splashing of bodily fluids during certain procedures;
Prevent contamination from a health worker's clothing onto the patient;
Stop irritation from the spillage of drugs, chemicals or other substances;
Keep user's clothes clean;
Uniforms with fluid repellent material provide greater body cover and freedom of movement.

Masks
Protect the mucous membranes of the user from any splashing or aerosol spray of bodily fluids from the patient;
Provide a barrier from the transmission of infectious droplets from close contact with patients.

Safety spectacles / goggles / shields
Protect the eyes from chemical splashes or spray of bodily fluids;
Provide a barrier to flying dirt or debris as a result of a procedure being carried-out.

Foot protection
Fully covered sturdy footwear with reinforced toe area protects from dropping of heavy items and also from spillages of chemicals or discharge of bodily fluids.

Risk Assessment
The Practice should carry-out regular and thorough risk assessments.

As we have already identified, PPE should always be used as the last resort when the other possible 5 measures (identified in the introduction) have been exhausted or deemed not practical or possible to do.

When the use of PPE has been identified as the correct course of action, the following points must be considered in relation to the correct type, situation, environment, quantity and fit:
The risk itself;
Which areas of the body could be affected;
The nature of the procedure or task;
Degree of physical effort involved;
Methods of work;
Duration of the procedure (therefore: how long a period of time must the PPE be used);
Personal needs / requests of the PPE user;
Does the PPE prevent or reduce risk or could it actually be inadvertently effective in spreading infection?;
Is the PPE adjustable to fit the user - whoever that may be?;
Is the PPE compatible with other complementary forms of PPE?

Staff Training

All staff members who need to use PPE in the course of their duties must be fully trained in the types of PPE appropriate to the work they are doing, and when and how to use it.

This training will normally be provided by the PPE lead or other senior clinical member of staff at the Practice. ***Insert Name(s) & Position of Person(s)*** is / are responsible for PPE training at the Practice.

Training will include theoretical and practical aspects as to why PPE is being used; its benefits and limitations, how to store, fit, wear and dispose of it correctly. Complete records will be kept of all training provided (see Appendix A).

Roles and Responsibilities

Managers, partners and senior staff must reinforce the standards in this policy at all times - this should be cascaded to all members of staff and ensure that they understand and adhere to the requirements;

Any essential PPE or uniform identified from the results of a risk assessment must be made available to staff
Any necessary training is provided to ensure safe use of equipment
Updated training is provided when appropriate
Risk assessments reviewed every 12 months
Staff monitored to ensure compliance with training and risk assessments

Staff members must also be responsible for;

Adhering to practice standards for dress codes, uniforms and personal appearance at all times
Staying up to date with the requirements of this and other related policies
Attending any training that has been provided relating to the safe use of personal protective equipment
Notifying management if any provided or subsidised workwear/uniforms/PPE needs replacing.

Coronavirus Updates - PPE in Primary Care
Update 21 May 2020
The following information has been taken from primary care guidance published by Public Health England
HSE has examined the use of FFP2 respirators as an alternative to Type IIR surgical face masks in non-surgical settings
If risk assessment identifies a need for FFP2 respirators the user must be face fit tested to ensure they are providing the intended level of protection
in circumstances where a lower level of user protection is required, such as that provided by a surgical mask, an FFP2 worn without a face fit test will offer protection like the levels from a surgical face mask
This is a pragmatic approach for times of severe shortage of respiratory protective equipment (RPE), FFP2 respirators being used in this way will not be carrying out the function they were designed to perform.
Where your risk assessment has identified the requirement for a tight-fitting respirator, users must pass a face fit test for that respirator model before it can be used.
Employers and users of respirators need to be assured protective equipment is protecting the wearer.
Note: Surgical masks may sometimes be used for source control, if feasible and if the mask can be tolerated by the individual; for example, a surgical mask worn by a patient with COVID-19 during transfer within a hospital. When an FFP2 respirator needs to be used in place of a surgical mask for this specific purpose, it should an unvalved FFP2 respirator (that is, no exhale valve).
The Health and Safety Executive (HSE) has reviewed that where there are acute shortages of PPE (and where it is safe to do so), it approves the sessional and reuse of PPE. Some of the PPE in the NHS COVID-19 Ensemble is designated by the manufacturers as being single use. However, the HSE recognises that some compromise is needed to optimise the supply of PPE in times of extreme
shortages. It has agreed that the use as outlined in this document is appropriate within health and safety legislation and provides appropriate protection for health and care workers.
These are exceptional circumstances and do not reflect HSE's standard approach. HSE expects healthcare organisations to have an agreed action plan to support implementation which includes a consideration of all measures to manage usage effectively.
In addition, documentation of how any re-use will be managed (recognising that some PPE is personal, for example FFP3/FFP2 respirators) should include a record of systems of work to manage how integrity checks and decontamination processes are being carried out. It should also recognise that certain equipment (for example gloves and aprons) cannot be reused.
Practices should ensure healthcare workers are appropriately hydrated during prolonged use and trained to recognise dehydration, fatigue and exhaustion while wearing PPE.
