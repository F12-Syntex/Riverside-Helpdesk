CHAPERONE POLICY (Updated 2026)

Practice: Riverside Practice
Approved by: Dr R Goel ( GP Partner)
Policy Owner: Iqra Khan ( Practice Manager)

1. Introduction

This policy is designed to protect patients and staff during consultations and examinations and to ensure examinations are conducted in a safe, respectful and professional environment.

Riverside Practice recognises that the use of chaperones forms an important part of maintaining sexual safety, professional boundaries and patient dignity.

This policy supports the practice’s:

  • Sexual Misconduct and Sexual Safety Policy
  • NHS Sexual Safety Charter commitments
  • Chaperoning principles issued by NHS England (December 2025)

Chaperones help safeguard:

  • patient dignity
  • professional standards
  • clinicians and staff from misunderstanding or false allegations.

2. Definition of a Chaperone

A chaperone is a trained member of staff who is present during an examination or procedure to:

  • support the patient
  • safeguard the patient
  • act as an impartial observer
  • assist the clinician where appropriate
  • ensure professional standards are maintained.

A family member or friend cannot act as a formal chaperone, although they may remain present for reassurance if the patient wishes.

3. When a Chaperone Should Be Offered

A chaperone must be offered for all intimate examinations.

Intimate examinations include but are not limited to:

  • breast examinations
  • genital examinations
  • rectal examinations
  • pelvic examinations
  • procedures involving exposure of intimate areas.

The clinician should also consider offering a chaperone when:

  • the examination may be perceived as intimate
  • the patient appears uncomfortable
  • there may be a language barrier
  • the patient has learning difficulties
  • there is a history of trauma or vulnerability
  • cultural sensitivities may apply
  • the clinician feels a chaperone is appropriate.

Patients have the right to decline a chaperone, but this must be documented.

If the clinician feels uncomfortable proceeding without a chaperone, they may:

  • explain why a chaperone is recommended
  • delay the examination
  • refer the patient to another clinician if appropriate.

4. Chaperones for Children and Young People

For patients under the age of 18, a formal chaperone must be present for intimate examinations.

A parent, guardian or carer may also be present if the young person wishes, but this does not replace the need for a trained chaperone.

Safeguarding considerations must always be taken into account.

5. Professional Conduct During Examination

Clinicians must:

  • explain the examination clearly before starting
  • obtain patient consent
  • maintain dignity and privacy
  • allow the patient privacy to undress and dress
  • use appropriate draping where possible
  • maintain a professional manner at all times.

Humour or comments that may be misinterpreted should be avoided.

6. Offering a Chaperone

Patients must be made aware that they can request a chaperone.

This may be achieved through:

  • signage in consulting rooms
  • patient information materials
  • verbal explanation by clinicians.

If a patient requests a chaperone:

  • the examination must not proceed without one
  • if none are available, the appointment should be rearranged unless clinically urgent.

7. Who Can Act as a Chaperone

Where possible, chaperones should be clinical staff trained in the role.

Appropriate staff may include:

  • Practice Nurses
  • Healthcare Assistants
  • other trained clinical staff.

Where non-clinical staff act as chaperones:

  • the patient must agree to this
  • the staff member must have received appropriate training
  • they must understand their responsibilities.

All chaperones must be:

  • trained in the role
  • aware of professional boundaries
  • confident in raising concerns if necessary.

8. Role of the Chaperone

The chaperone should:

  • introduce themselves to the patient
  • explain their role
  • remain present throughout the examination
  • position themselves so they can observe the procedure
  • respect patient dignity and confidentiality.

The chaperone should not:

  • participate in conversation unless appropriate
  • interfere with the consultation unless a concern arises.

If the chaperone witnesses inappropriate behaviour or has concerns, they must raise this immediately with the Practice Manager or Lead GP in line with the Sexual Misconduct and Sexual Safety Policy.

9. Documentation

The clinician must record in the patient record:

  • that a chaperone was offered
  • whether the patient accepted or declined
  • the name and role of the chaperone
  • if declined, that the patient declined.

If the patient declines a chaperone but the clinician proceeds with the examination, the notes should reflect this clearly.

If the patient requests a chaperone and none is available, the consultation should be rescheduled unless clinically urgent.

Chaperones should record any concerns or incidents that occur during the examination.

10. Confidentiality

The chaperone must respect the patient’s right to confidentiality.

The chaperone should only be present during the examination and leave once the examination is complete unless otherwise requested.

All staff must follow practice confidentiality policies.

11. Home Visits

Occasionally, intimate examinations may be required during home visits.

Where possible:

  • a chaperone should be arranged in advance
  • if not possible, the clinician should assess risk carefully
  • documentation must clearly record the circumstances.

If the clinician feels a chaperone is necessary but one cannot be arranged, the examination should be postponed unless clinically urgent.

12. Concerns or Allegations

If a patient or staff member raises a concern about conduct during an examination, this must be reported immediately to:

  • Practice Manager
  • Lead GP
  • Freedom to Speak Up Guardian

The matter will be handled in line with the practice’s Sexual Misconduct and Sexual Safety Policy, and may involve escalation to:

  • NHS England
  • safeguarding authorities
  • professional regulators
  • the police where appropriate.

13. Training

All staff acting as chaperones must receive training covering:

  • the role and responsibilities of a chaperone
  • patient dignity
  • safeguarding awareness
  • professional boundaries
  • responding to concerns.

Training records will be maintained by the Practice Manager.

14. Monitoring and Review

This policy will be reviewed annually or earlier if:

  • NHS guidance changes
  • legislation changes
  • incidents indicate a need for revision.

15. Equality and Diversity

Riverside Practice recognises that cultural, religious and personal beliefs may affect a patient’s preferences regarding examinations and chaperones.

Where possible, reasonable adjustments will be made to respect patient preferences while maintaining safe clinical practice.
