Triage Guidelines for Riverside Practice Receptionists

Conditions Managed by Pharmacists and Their Symptoms

Common Cold

  • Symptoms: Sneezing, runny or blocked nose, sore throat, cough, mild fever, headache.
  • Exceptions: Patients with high fever (above 38°C), breathing difficulties, or symptoms lasting more than 10 days. Infants (under 2 years) or elderly patients (over 75 years).

Coughs and Sore Throats

  • Symptoms: Persistent cough, hoarse voice, pain or irritation in the throat.
  • Exceptions: Severe pain, difficulty swallowing, or if accompanied by a high fever. Patients with a history of asthma, COPD, or other chronic breathing problems.

Hay Fever and Allergies

  • Symptoms: Sneezing, itchy eyes, runny nose, itchy throat.
  • Exceptions: Severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis) with swelling of the face/lips/tongue, difficulty breathing, or sudden rash. Patients with asthma made worse by allergies.

Indigestion and Heartburn

  • Symptoms: Burning sensation in the chest, bloating, nausea, belching.
  • Exceptions: Severe chest pain, especially if spreading to arm/jaw (could be a heart attack). Patients with a history of heart disease.

Diarrhoea

  • Symptoms: Frequent, loose, or watery stools, abdominal cramps.
  • Exceptions: Severe dehydration signs (dizziness, dark urine), blood in stool, or diarrhoea lasting more than 48 hours. Infants (under 2 years), elderly (over 75 years), or immunocompromised patients.

Constipation

  • Symptoms: Infrequent bowel movements, hard or painful stools, bloating.
  • Exceptions: Severe abdominal pain, vomiting, or if symptoms last more than two weeks. Patients with conditions like diabetes or thyroid disorders.

Mild Skin Conditions

  • Conditions: Eczema, contact dermatitis, insect bites, minor burns, and sunburn.
  • Symptoms: Red, itchy, or inflamed skin, rash, blisters.
  • Exceptions: Severe burns, large areas of skin affected, or signs of infection (pus, spreading redness). Patients with diabetes (risk of infection).

Head Lice

  • Symptoms: Itchy scalp, visible nits or lice.
  • Exceptions: Severe scalp irritation or infection. Very young children (under 2 years).

Athlete’s Foot

  • Symptoms: Itchy, red, or scaly rash between toes or on feet, blisters.
  • Exceptions: Severe infection or if the patient is diabetic (risk of complications).

Threadworms

  • Symptoms: Itchy bottom, especially at night, visible worms in stools.
  • Exceptions: Severe itching or if the patient has a weakened immune system.

Vaginal Thrush

  • Symptoms: Itching and soreness around the vagina, thick white discharge, discomfort during urination or sex.
  • Exceptions: Recurrent thrush (more than four times a year), severe symptoms, or if the patient is pregnant.

Cold Sores

  • Symptoms: Tingling, itching, or burning around the lips, followed by blisters.
  • Exceptions: Severe, widespread cold sores or if the patient is immunocompromised.

General Exceptions

  1. Age:
    • Infants: Under 2 years old.
    • Elderly: Over 75 years old.
  2. Severity of Illness: Any patient who looks very unwell, with symptoms like high fever, difficulty breathing, severe pain, or signs of dehydration, should see a GP or go to emergency services.
  3. Chronic Conditions: Patients with serious long-term illnesses (e.g., heart disease, diabetes, COPD) should be checked by a GP to ensure they get the right care.
  4. Immunocompromised Patients: Patients with weak immune systems due to conditions like HIV, cancer, or recent organ transplant should see a GP even for minor ailments.

Steps for Receptionists

  1. Assess Symptoms: Listen carefully to the patient's symptoms and compare them to the conditions listed.
  2. Identify Exceptions: Be careful about exceptions, including age, severity of symptoms, and chronic health conditions.
  3. Provide Clear Guidance: Tell patients which service is best for their symptoms. Reassure them if they need to see the GP or go to urgent care.
  4. Escalate When Necessary: If unsure, be cautious and direct the patient to see a GP or seek emergency care.

Importance of This Guide

  • Patient Safety: Makes sure patients who need quick or special care get it on time.
  • Efficient Use of Resources: Helps use healthcare resources effectively by guiding patients to the right level of care.
  • Improved Patient Outcomes: Early and correct care for more serious conditions can greatly improve patient health.

Follow-Up and Queries

Encourage patients to come back if their symptoms persist or get worse, despite the treatment provided by the pharmacist.

Recognising Urgent Symptoms

Receptionists should know specific keywords and phrases indicating a patient may need urgent medical attention. Here are some common symptoms, the associated language, and important indicators to watch for:

Adults (18 years and over)

  • Chest Pain:
    • Keywords: "tightness", "pressure", "pain in chest", "heart pain"
  • Shortness of Breath (SOB):
    • Keywords: "difficulty breathing", "can't catch my breath", "wheezing"
  • Severe Adverse Reactions:
    • Keywords: "swelling", "rash", "allergic reaction", "hives"
  • Signs of Sepsis:
    • Keywords: "fever", "shivering", "very cold", "confused", "slurred speech"
  • Blood Pressure/Pulse Extremes:
    • High BP: Above 180/120
    • High Pulse: Above 110
    • Low BP: Below 110/70
    • Low Pulse: Below 60
  • High Temperature:
    • Keywords: "fever", "very hot", "burning up"
    • Important Indicator: Above 40°C that is not reducing
  • Severe Headache:
    • Keywords: "worst headache", "can't handle the pain"
  • Severe Abdominal Pain:
    • Keywords: "stomach pain", "cramping", "severe pain"
  • Sudden Weakness or Numbness:
    • Keywords: "can't move", "weak arm/leg", "numbness"

Children Under 5

  • Keywords: "not eating or drinking", "very sleepy", "won't wake up", "persistent crying", “rash that does not go when pressed’’
  • Important Indicators:
    • High fever (> 38°C)
    • Persistent crying
    • Lethargy
    • Refusal to eat or drink for over 24 hours
    • Not passed urine for 6 hours

Children Over 5

  • Keywords: "fever", "feeling very weak", "difficulty breathing", "severe pain"
  • Important Indicators:
    • High fever (> 39°C
    • Severe headache
    • Vomiting or diarrhoea
    • Not responding to pain relief
    • Not passed urine for 6 hours

Key Age Groups and High-Risk Populations

Elderly Patients

  • Important Indicators: Confusion, dizziness, falls, severe pain, difficulty breathing, high BP (> 180/120), low BP (< 110/70), high pulse (> 110), low pulse (< 60).

Chronic Illness Patients

  • Important Indicators: Worsening symptoms in diabetes, heart disease, respiratory issues, or immunocompromised conditions.

Questions to Ask

  • Basic Information: "Can you describe your symptoms?"
  • Duration and Severity: "How long have you had these symptoms?", "How severe is the pain on a scale of 1 to 10?"
  • Recent Changes: "Have your symptoms gotten worse recently?"
  • Associated Symptoms: "Do you have any other symptoms like fever, nausea, or dizziness?"

Decision-Making Process

  • Urgent Escalation:
    • If the caller mentions any of the keywords or phrases indicating severe symptoms.
    • If the caller belongs to a high-risk age group or has a chronic illness with worsening symptoms.
    • If specific values for BP, pulse, or temperature fall into critical ranges.
    • Immediately escalate to the on-call doctor.
    • Calls from external parties (e.g. doctor/nurse ambulance/ social services/ coroners) requesting to speak to a Duty Doctor urgently, ambulance, social services or coroners. Take down caller details (including contact number) and call Duty Doctor.
  • Non-Urgent:
    • For symptoms that are mild, not worsening, or have been persistent but stable.
    • Book a pre-booked appointment with their regular GP.

Example Conversations for Receptionists

  • Urgent Cases:
    • "I understand you are experiencing [specific symptom]. This sounds serious, and I will escalate this to our on-call doctor immediately."
    • "Please hold while I connect you with the duty doctor."
  • Non-Urgent Cases:
    • "Based on what you've told me, I will book you a telephone appointment with X on [date/time]"
    • "I will send your doctor a message, who will contact you within a week – if they need to see you F2F, the reception team will notify you within 48 hours; otherwise, expect a call. "
    • "If your symptoms worsen before your appointment, please call us back immediately."

Routine Queries

Follow-Ups/Flare-Ups

  • Action: Send a routine task for ongoing symptoms which have not improved, to their original GP providing their symptoms do not fall into the urgent queries list (follow protocol if urgent) and their symptoms have not gotten worse.
  • If symptoms worsen: Book an appointment with the next available GP.
  • Dr Goel can follow up unwell patients when he is on duty Mon to Wed only

Test Results

  • Patient requests for test results: Send a task to the doctor involved in the case.
  • If the original doctor is away: Routine tasks can still be sent to the original doctor as some time is allocated for task completion.

Sick Note Requests/Extensions

  • For the first 7 days of illness, patients must self-certify online (https://www.gov.uk/guidance/ask-your-employer-for-statutory-sick-pay).
  • New sick note requests without medical evidence: Book a routine appointment with the next available GP.
  • New sick note requests with medical evidence: (A+E letter/NHS 111 report, seen by FCP) and has been over 7 days – if we have proof, send routine task to RG, If RG is away, then add to Duty Doctor task list.
  • Existing sick notes (including private sick notes): Send a task to the original doctor for an extension, including the dates the patient is requesting.

Referral Requests

  • If previous discussion with GP: Send a task to the same GP.
  • New referral requests without previous discussion: Book a routine appointment with the next available GP.

CPCS Outcomes (non-urgent)

  • Action: Send an urgent task to the duty doctor (DD) to review and advise on the next steps.

New Medical Problems/AccuRx (non-urgent)

  • Action: Book an appointment with the next available doctor after exhausting other services, such as CPCS, FCP, MEC, Hub.

Note: Aim to task requests to the doctor who is seeing the patient for the specific query to facilitate consistent clinical care. If the original doctor is away, routine tasks can still be sent to them due to the allocated time for task completion. New problems generally require an appointment. – This should be discussed with the duty doctor, to ensure it is safe to wait for the original doctor, contacting them through urgent task/screen messages depending on the doctor present.

Workflow Coding

Discharge Letters

  • Code: discharge letter emailed to GP
  • Usage: For all discharge letters. The date is the date of discharge

A&E Visits

  • Code: seen in A&E during surgery hours (0800 - 1830pm)
  • Usage: For documenting patient visits to A&E during surgery hours. The date is the date they arrived
  • Code: seen in A&E (out of hours)
  • Usage: For documenting patient visits to A&E outside of standard surgery hours. The date is the date they arrived.

Clinical Letters

  • Code: clinical letter (add dept or hospital name)
  • Usage: For all other clinical letters other than discharge letters. Include the department or hospital name. the date should be the date they were seen

Administrative Notes

  • Code: admin note
  • Usage: For non-clinical communications, letters from patients, INR readings, or any other administrative materials.
