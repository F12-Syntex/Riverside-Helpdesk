Urgent / Emergency Appointment Requests: Reception Guide

Policy

No matter what appointment system any surgery has in place, there will always be times when an emergency/urgent appointment is requested by a patient. It is therefore imperative that all receptionists are trained in and utilise a policy that is right for the surgery and applicable for all patients. It will obviously vary on the size of the practice list.

This is addressed by using the dedicated pink duty doctor slots that are used specifically for these emergencies, or there could be slots at the end of a doctor’s list on the morning and afternoon surgeries for these emergencies. It may also be necessary on occasion to insert a patient into the current list.

These appointment queries could be given to the duty doctor. 

There has been a move towards getting patients to send in an electronic consultation for an on-call doctor to determine the best triage way forward. That is a good way to gather facts and symptoms, ready for a review by the on-call doctor.

There is also a distinction between “emergency” and urgent, and care must be taken to ensure that “emergencies” are identified as such so that the patient may be correctly referred to A&E for emergency care. Urgent appointments are such that the nature of the patient’s medical condition requires that they be seen as soon as possible. They are not usually appropriate for items of an administrative nature, such as fit notes.

It will be the case that the receptionist will need to make a decision as to what is urgent and what isn’t initially. ‘Urgent’ could encompass patients that are either very young or elderly, or a sudden illness or worsening of a current medical condition.

 When a patient calls asking for emergency/urgent appointment full details should be taken at that stage.

  • Name of Patient
  • Age of patient
  • Contact telephone number
  • Symptoms
  • How long they have had this current problem.
  • Who are they with?
  • If, as some surgeries still have an open surgery, why did they not come down to that?

At that point it should be possible to detect how urgent the situation is. It could be that it is something that would be best dealt with at a hospital, for example if symptoms include ‘short of breath with chest pains’, the  advice to either call 999 or go to A&E may well be the appropriate course of action. Where appropriate (e.g. patient is on their own or elderly) consideration should be given to calling an ambulance on the patient’s behalf. In these circumstances a doctor should be advised immediately.

It may well be that a patient that clearly needs an urgent appointment can be given one that day.

If it is deemed urgent and there are no appointments left that day then a message is taken for the duty doctor, or the doctor should be interrupted as appropriate. The doctor will make the final decision as to the extent of the emergency and if they will grant an emergency appointment.

If in any doubt it is better to err on the side of caution and leave a message for the duty doctor. See duty doctor protocol.

Recognising Urgent Symptoms

Receptionists should know specific keywords and phrases indicating a patient may need urgent medical attention. Here are some common symptoms, the associated language, and important indicators to watch for:

  1. Adults (18 years and over)
  • Chest Pain:
    • Keywords: "tightness", "pressure", "pain in chest", "heart pain"
  • Shortness of Breath (SOB):
    • Keywords: "difficulty breathing", "can't catch my breath", "wheezing"
  • Severe Adverse Reactions:
    • Keywords: "swelling", "rash", "allergic reaction", "hives"
  • Signs of Sepsis:
    • Keywords: "fever", "shivering", "very cold", "confused", "slurred speech"
  • Blood Pressure/Pulse Extremes:
    • High BP: Above 180/120
    • High Pulse: Above 110
    • Low BP: Below 110/70
    • Low Pulse: Below 60
  • High Temperature:
    • Keywords: "fever", "very hot", "burning up"
    • Important Indicator: Above 40°C that is not reducing
  • Severe Headache:
    • Keywords: "worst headache", "can't handle the pain"
  • Severe Abdominal Pain:
    • Keywords: "stomach pain", "cramping", "severe pain"
  • Sudden Weakness or Numbness:
    • Keywords: "can't move", "weak arm/leg", "numbness"
  1. Children Under 5
  • Keywords: "not eating or drinking", "very sleepy", "won't wake up", "persistent crying", “rash that does not go when pressed’’
  • Important Indicators:
    • High fever (> 38°C)
    • Persistent crying
    • Lethargy
    • Refusal to eat or drink for over 24 hours
    • Not passed urine for 6 hours
  1. Children Over 5
  • Keywords: "fever", "feeling very weak", "difficulty breathing", "severe pain"
  • Important Indicators:
    • High fever (> 39°C
    • Severe headache
    • Vomiting or diarrhoea
    • Not responding to pain relief
    • Not passed urine for 6 hours
  1. Key Age Groups and High-Risk Populations

Elderly Patients

  • Important Indicators: Confusion, dizziness, falls, severe pain, difficulty breathing, high BP (> 180/120), low BP (< 110/70), high pulse (> 110), low pulse (< 60).

Chronic Illness Patients

  • Important Indicators: Worsening symptoms in diabetes, heart disease, respiratory issues, or immunocompromised conditions.
  1. Questions to Ask
  • Basic Information: "Can you describe your symptoms?"
  • Duration and Severity: "How long have you had these symptoms?", "How severe is the pain on a scale of 1 to 10?"
  • Recent Changes: "Have your symptoms gotten worse recently?"
  • Associated Symptoms: "Do you have any other symptoms like fever, nausea, or dizziness?"
  1. Decision-Making Process
  • Urgent Escalation:
    • If the caller mentions any of the keywords or phrases indicating severe symptoms.
    • If the caller belongs to a high-risk age group or has a chronic illness with worsening symptoms.
    • If specific values for BP, pulse, or temperature fall into critical ranges.
    • Immediately escalate to the duty doctor.
    • Calls from external parties (e.g. doctor/nurse ambulance/ social services/ coroners) requesting to speak to a Duty Doctor urgently, ambulance, social services or coroners. Take down caller details (including contact number) and call Duty Doctor.
  • Non-Urgent:
    • For symptoms that are mild, not worsening, or have been persistent but stable.
    • Book a pre-booked appointment with their regular GP.
  1. Example Conversations for Receptionists
  • Urgent Cases:
    • "I understand you are experiencing [specific symptom]. This sounds serious, and I will escalate this to our duty doctor immediately."
    • "Please hold while I connect you with the duty doctor."
  • Non-Urgent Cases:
    • "Based on what you've told me, I will book you a telephone appointment with X on [date/time]"
    • "I will send your doctor a message, who will contact you within a week – if they need to see you F2F, the reception team will notify you within 48 hours; otherwise, expect a call. "
    • "If your symptoms worsen before your appointment, please call us back immediately."
