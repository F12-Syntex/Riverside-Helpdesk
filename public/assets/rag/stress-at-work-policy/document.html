THE RIVERSIDE PRACTICE
STRESS AT WORK POLICY
Introduction
The practice recognises that pressure of change and work may lead to stress in any of its employees.

The practice will, so far as is reasonably practical, work to identify, assess and minimise factors which could lead to physical or psychological stress-related illness, as well as continually reviewing working practices as part of the assessments process and encouraging employees to report any significant symptoms to ensure help and support is given at an early stage.

Applicability
This policy applies to all employees and partners. The practice will endeavour to ensure that other people who work at the practice, e.g. self-employed staff, temporary staff and contractors, do not suffer stress as a result of working at the practice by implementing this policy where applicable for non-employed workers.

What Is Stress?
Most people experience pressure at some point in their lives, and indeed some pressures can be regarded as positive and part of the everyday working life. People are better able to withstand pressure and tension in some circumstances than others, and all have their own point at which pressure can turn into stress or illness.

It is important to recognise when this boundary is crossed and reasonable pressures lead to distress and illness. The causes of stress are many; the home/work relationship, working conditions, work demands, change and uncertainty, lack of job security, life events, personal relationships etc.

What to Do If You Feel Stressed
Raise any issues of concern with the Practice Manager or ask a colleague to do so. Work with the Practice to address any stressors and create a plan to address the situation.

Potential Sources of Stress
Workplace: work overload/underload; poor job design; role ambiguity; conflict/relationship breakdowns; people management; organisational change; job insecurity; bullying and harassment; change in pace of technology.

Personal: Unhealthy eating; sleeping or exercise; general health; personal relationships; significant life events; changes to the economy.
Potential Implications of Stress
Harmful pressure or distress manifests itself in a wide range of psychological, physiological and behavioural consequences.

Psychological symptoms: anxiety; irritability; frustration; depression; inability to concentrate; procrastination; inability to make decisions; feelings of unreality.

Physical symptoms: muscular tension; headaches and palpitations; high blood pressure; digestive problems or increased susceptibility to colds, influenza or respiratory problems.

Behavioural symptoms: sub-optimum performance and productivity; higher levels of absence; arriving late to work; leaving work early; taking extended lunch breaks; tobacco, alcohol, caffeine or other substance abuse; negative personal appearance and hygiene changes; weight loss or gain.

Managing Stress
Awareness - What causes you stress and how do you react?

Balance - How much can you cope with, what helps you feel satisfied, successful and secure?

Control - What helps you feel more control, what steps can you take to improve your situation?

It is a great strength to know yourself and your capabilities. Admitting that you are under stress and not coping well is strength and not a weakness.

Try to prevent the build up of stress by:

Improving your work process
Using your time wisely and balancing work and home life

Maintaining good healthy habits - eat well, exercise regularly, have sufficient sleep and learn how to relax
Make decisions
Practical Support
The practice will support staff to reduce individual vulnerabilities including:

Providing a support network
Providing information on occupational health and counselling services
Encourage healthy habits, including diet, sleep and exercise
Developing coping techniques, including assertiveness and time management
Access to relaxation techniques including massage, aromatherapy and other complementary therapies

The practice will endeavour to assist any employee suffering with stress by:

Rebalancing workloads
Giving clarification of what is required in the role
Evaluating communications and change process
Evaluation of management and supervisory process
Provide relevant training
Address any bullying and harassment within the Practice
Recognise the need to support staff on sickness absence due to a stress-related illness to return to work in a sympathetic manner
Educate managers and partners to recognise and respond to distress
Create an action plan to address any stressors

Above all else, the company wants to create an open and honest culture, where employees feel able to talk about such issues as stress to their manager, so the causes and effects can be successfully minimised and managed.
Tools
The Health and Safety Executive have produced a number of tools that can be used to address, identify and prevent stress. Where possible these will be considered including:
Managing and Communicating Existing and Future Work (Employer)
Management Standards Indicator Tool (Employee)
Action Plan Template
Stress Risk Assessment/Return to Work Discussion

Improving GPs' Access to Mental Health Support (NHS England)

As part of the Five Year Forward View, a new national service is being established by NHS England to improve GPs' access to mental health support. The service is expected to be available across England from December 2016. This means all GPs will be able to access free, confidential local support and treatment for mental health issues, supporting GPs who are at risk of suffering stress or burnout.

Data Protection
The practice will ensure that individuals' personal data, including information about their mental health, collected when managing stress is handled in accordance with its Data Protection Policy and Employee Privacy Notice.
