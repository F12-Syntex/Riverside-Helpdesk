THE RIVERSIDE PRACTICE
HIV/AIDS POLICY (STAFF)
Introduction

AIDS is a disease caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), which attacks the body's natural defence system and leaves it open to various infections and cancers. With early diagnosis and effective treatment, most people with HIV will not go on to develop AIDS. HIV infection is not acquired through everyday social and work contact, or sharing cutlery or toilets with an infected person. Nor can it be caught in the same way as flu or colds, via coughs and sneezes. The main means of transmission are:
Unprotected sexual intercourse with an infected person
Taking infected blood into the bloodstream, for example by using contaminated syringes or needles for drug injection
From an infected mother to her unborn child
Applicability

The policy applies to all employees and Partners, and also applies to other people who work at the practice e.g. self-employed staff, temporary staff and contractors.
The Policy

Employees with AIDS or who are HIV positive and whose performance suffers or is absent because of their illness will be treated in the same way as individuals with any other long-term condition or disability.
The practice will adopt a non-discriminatory policy in accordance with the Equality Act 2010 provisions and ethos.
Employees are expected to work normally with a colleague who has or is suspected of having AIDS or who is HIV positive - it is a disciplinary offence for an employee to refuse to work normally with a colleague with AIDS or who is HIV positive.
Any breaches of an affected employee's right to non-discrimination, confidentiality and privacy will be treated as a disciplinary offence.
Any disciplinary offence will be handled through the Practice's Disciplinary Procedure [*].
Normally, no employee will be asked to take a test for HIV antibodies. An exception would be for members of staff who will be performing exposure-prone procedures (EPPs), in which case additional health clearance must be undertaken. This means being checked and non-infectious for HIV (antibody negative) - in the context of general practice however, the need for this this may be rare - minor surgical procedures such as excision of sebaceous cysts, skin lesions, cauterization of skin warts, aspiration of bursae, cortisone injections into joints and

vasectomies do not usually constitute EPPs. Current procedures for determining medical fitness on recruitment will continue to apply.
Public Health England guidance advises that HIV positive staff can perform EPP work in some circumstances, which includes being on appropriate treatment and management, with a plasma viral load level of less than 200 copies per ml. Further information can be found here.
Any non-clinical employee who becomes infected with HIV, or who develops AIDS is not required to inform the Practice unless he/she wishes to do so. However, current rules for certification of sickness absence will continue to apply.
Clinical staff who become infected with HIV, or who develop AIDS, must follow their own professional guidance on disclosure.
Any member of staff needing help may seek advice through the Practice Manager or via Occupational Health.
If any disclosure about an individual who has AIDS/HIV is necessary, it will only take place after authorisation has been sought from the person concerned.
No employee will be redeployed solely because he/she has AIDS/HIV, unless it is in the interests of their own safety or that of others.

Data Protection
The practice will ensure that individuals' personal data, including data relating to their health, is handled in accordance with its Data Protection Policy. Any breach of confidentiality will be treated very seriously and dealt with under the organisation's disciplinary procedure.
Procedures for First Aid

First Aiders should be given up to date training and advice on HIV. There have been no known cases of infection transmitted by administration of first aid measures and usual hygiene precautions will protect against HIV.

Ensure Universal Procedures for First Aid are followed:
Wear gloves and other PPE where possible
Ensure hands are cleaned prior and after administering first aid
Be attentive to avoid contact with blood, body fluids, or soiled items, where possible.
Be careful of sharp objects, such as broken glass, near the injured person
Cover cuts or open skin with a clean and dry dressing.
