The Riverside Practice
Policy for Summarising Patient Records

The practice is a paperless practice and in order to ensure the quality of the computer records the practice has a clear policy for summarising paper records.

The practice nurse has received in-house training form the lead GP and is the person responsible for summarising all patient records once received. Several important rules are followed:

The correct date of any diagnosis is entered
The most accurate Read Code is used starting from the top code of that stem.
Significant Active is used for ongoing conditions requiring treatment or affecting treatment (e.g. asthma)
Significant Past is used for operations and invasive procedures and Allergies and major illnesses, eg malaria, TB.
Minor Past is used for minor illnesses

Selection criteria for conditions entered on the computer.

Significant
Any long term condition, e.g. asthma, heart disease, diabetes
Any life threatening condition, e.g. cancer, heart disease.
Any major operations, e.g. C.A.B.G.
Any condition that could have long-term implications, e.g. peptic ulcer, mental health problems, drug addiction/abuse.
Any significant social history, e.g. children on the Child Protection Register.

Minor
Any condition that the patient will recover from with no on-going problems e.g. Pregnancy with normal delivery, tonsillectomy, appendectomy.

Active
Any on-going condition, e.g. recent operations, current illness.
Any long term or life threatening condition, e.g. diabetes, mental health problems
Any significant social history, e.g. Child Protection Register.
Any ALLERGIES coding as ALLERGY SNOMED code being as specific as possible, e.g "Allergy to Penicillin"

Significant Past
Any condition which is no longer a current problem but may have implications for the future, e.g. mastectomy, hip replacement, drug/alcohol abuse.
Minor Past
Any condition which the patient has recovered from with no expected long term problems, e.g. simple fractures, tonsillectomy, appendectomy.

N.B. These are only general guidelines as each patient is individual and therefore there will be exceptions to these examples.

Allergies

These include drug and non drug (eg food) and are entered as a Significant Past problem on the problem page using the allergy function in EMIS Web. This ensures that the allergy is clearly visible to any person viewing the patient's records as well as appearing as a warning in the prescribing page.

Immunisations

All immunisations for under 5 year olds are recorded to identify those who need boosters and the practice administrator writes to the parents/guardian.

Smears

The date and result of the last cervical smear in women aged 25 to 65 is entered in the smear CEG template. If recall or referral is required then the doctor is informed who actions this.

Family History

Relevant family history is also entered under the Family History section.

In line with the quality and outcomes framework the national agreed Read Codes for chronic diseases included in the clinical domains are used and importantly the correct date of any diagnosis.

If the nurse has any queries or problems then the lead GP (Dr R Goel) is consulted for advice re coding of the manual data. If an action is identified after summarising the manual records then the lead GP is informed.

Once the notes have been summarised then the Read Code 9344 (Notes Summary on Computer) is added as a Significant Active problem so that it is clearly visible on the front page of the EMIS computer record.

The Lloyd George Notes

Once a set of notes is received by the practice this is coded on the EMIS system on the registration page and the notes given to the practice nurse for summarising onto the computer as above. Once summarised onto the computer the notes are then tagged and ordered in date order in 3 sections, clinical notes, letters and results. A red label is put on the spine of the file for identification as being tagged and summarised and then these are filed alphabetically.

New written information is first scanned into the computer records after being actioned by the doctor and then filed in the Lloyd George notes, so that these contain a complete set of all the written records of the patient.
