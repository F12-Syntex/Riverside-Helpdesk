THE RIVERSIDE PRACTICE
PORTABLE APPLIANCE TESTING (PAT) POLICY

INTRODUCTION

The legislation of specific relevance to electrical maintenance is the Health & Safety at Work Act 1974, the Management of Health & Safety at Work Regulations 1999, the Electricity at Work Regulations 1989, the Workplace (Health, Safety and Welfare) Regulations 1992 and the Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998.

The practice has a responsibility to demonstrate compliance with all appropriate guidelines and legislation.

Portable Appliances

There are many definitions and European standards for equipment but the definition of "portable" is not clear. As a guide this will cover:

Hand held items which can be used connected to a supply
Items intended to be moved while connected to a supply
Items capable of movement while connected to a supply
Items capable of being recharged while connected to a supply

It would be normal for these items to be connected via a plug and socket; however this is not necessarily a requirement before an item can fall within this category.

Examples are:

Floor cleaners, polishers, vacuum cleaners, kettles, fan heaters, televisions, extension leads, lamps etc.
It also covers equipment not normally designated as "portable" such as monitors, photocopiers, desktop computers, servers, fax machines etc.
Items of 18kg in mass, or less, intended, or which can easily be moved while in use (i.e. not fixed in position). It may however include domestic appliances such as fridges.
Clinical items, e.g. ECG machine, chargers (e.g. otoscopes/auroscopes, etc.), centrifuges, nebulisers, any non-battery powered electrical items (NB PAT testing is NOT calibration, simply a test to confirm items are electrically safe.

Policy

The practice will employ a firm specialising in Portable Appliance Testing to undertake testing on an annual basis. The regulations do not however require PAT testing to be on an annual basis in a low-risk environment, and not every item of equipment requires a test if it is visually examined, however the annual test is regarded as being a prudent "regular" interval as is required under the regulations and will apply in the practice.

Although the regulations do not preclude staff from testing the equipment if suitably trained it is considered that the use of an external specialist is essential in order to demonstrate competency and independence.

The practice will make all equipment and rooms within the building accessible to the tester during the period of the visit (including attic / storage spaces), and will ensure that any equipment held off the premises (nebulisers on loan, digital projectors, laptops etc.) are returned to the practice for the day of the test.

The company used for the test will normally:

Use testing equipment to test for earth and insulation
Visually check connecting cables and the internal security of non-sealed plugs
Shock-testing and fuse testing / rating of all plugs, with the correct use of cord grips.
Visually inspect for damage
Label each item with a unique identifier label, indicating the date of testing, and the date on which the next test is due
Fully log all equipment, nature, location, tester name and the test results and hold a central copy, providing a copy to the practice for the Health & Safety record file
Only power-down computer equipment with the express permission of a senior member of staff
Remove and immediately decommission / dispose of faulty items, after informing a senior member of staff
Certify compliance in accordance with the recommendations of the appropriate legislation

General Electrical Equipment Precautions

Under health and safety law, all employees have a duty of care of their own health and safety and that of others. Before using any electrical equipment all staff should undertake a "user check", and inspect both the appliance and the cable and socket to which it is connected for any obvious signs of wear or tear. Any concerns should be immediately raised with your line manager.
The frequency of inspection and testing depends upon the type of equipment and the environment it is used in. For example, a clinician's examination tool used on a patient should be examined more frequently than a lamp in an office.

Faulty or suspect equipment will be immediately removed from service and labelled as "Faulty" clearly. These items will be disposed of as soon as it is determined that they will not be economically repaired
All wall sockets will be kept clear and easily accessible to enable equipment to be switched off in an emergency
Unused sockets in patient areas to be protected by means of a "blanking plug"
Appropriate electrical equipment will be permanently wired in if appropriate
"Doubling" plugs will not be used. Extension multi-socket leads will be used as a last resort and will be correctly rated and fused, and preferably switched / surge protected.
All light fittings and bulbs will be correctly watts rated for the fitting
Appliances and sockets will be switched off before plugging-in or unplugging
Sufficient sockets will be provided for equipment to negate the need for extension cables
