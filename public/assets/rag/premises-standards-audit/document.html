PREMISES STANDARDS AUDIT
Introduction

Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) Regulations 2014: Regulation 15 sets the overall standard for safe premises in Primary Care and the CQC describe it as follows …. " The intention of this regulation is to make sure that the premises where care and treatment are delivered are clean, suitable for the intended purpose, maintained and where required, appropriately located, and that the equipment that is used to deliver care and treatment is clean, suitable for the intended purpose, maintained, stored securely and used properly. Providers retain legal responsibility under these regulations when they delegate responsibility through contracts or legal agreements to a third party, independent suppliers, professionals, supply chains or contractors. They must therefore make sure that they meet the regulation, as responsibility for any shortfall rests with the provider."
This means quite simply that anything premises-related that happens in a premises occupied by a Practice is the responsibility of the Practice. Landlords, contractors and any shared services within a premises must be held to account by the Practice and performance must be to the service level required. Ensuring that a Landlord - even if it is the NHS Property Services - is fulfilling their duty to a Practice by ensuring gas and electricity are safe, premises are clean and no-one will fall ill suffering from a Legionella infection falls fairly and squarely in the responsibility of the Practice. Likewise, in a Practice-owned premises any contractors who service the building must be made to perform to the required level and in both cases it is up to the Practice to know what standards are required and indeed what they must be monitoring.
This guidance document seeks to point Practice Managers in the right direction but is not exhaustive. Those responsible for managing practice accommodation must always maintain a keen knowledge of the obligations as well as ensure they master the detail both in their NHS Contract and in constantly changing and extensive legal and regulatory necessities.

Infection control guidance Facilities available
- Treatment Room(s) of sufficient size and equipped. Flooring of washable material
- Waiting room of sufficient size and with enough seating to accommodate patients under normal conditions
- Seating wipe-clean and undamaged
- Consulting rooms properly equipped
- Modesty curtains in all rooms where patients undress
- Furniture in good condition
- Noticeboards infection control compliant
- Hand gel available everywhere - especially communal areas
- General tidiness good
- Consulting & Treatment rooms with hot and cold running water and lever-type or sensor taps
- Somewhere for patients to have confidential conversations with staff
- Reception desk confidentiality reviewed? - waiting patients kept far enough away not to overhear?
NHS Guidance on the factors needed in calculating the number of consulting rooms is:
• number of weeks the building will be open per
year;
• opening hours per week;
• average duration of each appointment by service and room type;
• average room utilisation rate.

Reception areas present many problems - not least of which the ergonomics of the workstation and the placing of the computer screen(s) to prevent unauthorised viewing of any display General & Safety issues
- Health & Safety Risk Assessment done, documented and actions taken
- Doormats placed to reduce wet flooring in inclement weather
- Arrangements in place to grit/salt in cold weather
- Emergency lighting working and regularly checked
- Fire Risk Assessment done, documented and actions taken
- Extinguishers tested
- Gas and Electrical Safety Certificates current (Mains Electric every 5 yrs: Gas Annual)
- Legionella Risk Assessment done and action taken/ regular checks made on water temperature
- Asbestos Survey needed and done/available?
- PAT Testing completed
- Emergency call system for staff available and working
- Emergency Oxygen store has warning sign on outside of door
- Consulting rooms and record rooms secured against unauthorised access
- Electrical sockets in public areas sited to minimise child access to live terminals (NB: A CAS alert on 30 June 2016 advises against fitting child socket guards)
- No trailing wires/extensions
- Boiler and Electrical distribution rooms properly hazard signposted
- Natural ventilation and light available (but infection-control compliant?)
- First Aid kit available
- Clinical waste bins Infection control compliant
- Clinical waste storage area properly secured and signed as a Biological Hazard
- Access secured to rooms containing vaccine/pharmacy fridges
- Adequate controls to prevent conversations being overheard
Much of this is covered in other protocol areas, however this checklist may just serve to prompt an issue that has been overlooked or may generate further investigation into what is required.

GP Premises need to be flexible in their usage; however when a Consulting Room is used as a Treatment room, for example, the higher standard must apply.

Security in the premises and for equipment is very important and in particular there must be control over confidential conversations being inadvertently overheard.

Many Surgeries use television or radio to mask sounds coming from consulting rooms etc but expensive PPL and PRS licenses may be required. Cleanliness
- Modesty curtains disposed of/washed as per guidelines and dates recorded
- Separate area for `dirty` cleaning materials
- Security of storage of liquids etc. under COSHH
- Each room with a cleaning schedule and daily cleaning records.
- Special cleaning standards for treatment etc. rooms
- Air Conditioning/ Ventilation cleaned regularly Cleaning standards are covered in detail in the Infection Control guidelines. COSHH requirements do apply to external cleaning contractors and their COSHH folder should be available for all staff to consult. CCTV
- Can CCTV-covered areas shown on the VDU screens in different areas be seen only by Practice staff?
- Are images recorded and those recordings securely retained
- Do you have the required notice advertising your CCTV and how its` images can be obtained for `subject access`
- Is it maintained and working
- Are all relevant areas covered
(i.e. no blind spots)
- Are all covered areas appropriate for surveillance The provision of CCTV can be difficult as you cannot allow images collected elsewhere be seen by the public in another area. Images in any area where the person looking at the screen can see they are being picked up in that place are fine however.
CCTV is only lawful where it is sited for a clearly defined and appropriate purpose. External
- Are the grounds inspected frequently for hazards to staff and the public
- Are the grounds maintained regularly
- Windows cleaned
- Building fabric and roof inspected and maintained

A clear knowledge of the property boundary limits of the practice is essential when considering who is responsible for what. A CQC Inspection will note your external appearance before anything else on the day.

The owners as well as the users of the building have responsibilities The part played by others
- Do you have a documented system to capture public complaints and suggestions
- Can you show you have responded to those issues
- Do you have a robust maintenance agreement with your Landlord and a documented mechanism to report and monitor issues
- In a shared building does the Landlord organise Fire Evacuation Drills for ALL tenants? Where appropriate - knowing the contractual responsibilities of the Landlord and ensuring they stick to them will help to keep the working environment up to standard. Standards apply to staff matters in any well-led organisation Staff
- Are there changing/ showering facilities for the use of staff and are showers regularly checked against Legionella infection?
- Are there toilet facilities restricted for staff other than having to share with patients/public
- Is there a staff rest facility
- Is there a separate staff facility for making drinks/eating food rather than in their office or clinical room
- Do you have secure lockers or a secure facility for staff personal belongings/ handbags etc
- Have you clear guidelines for staff relating to hazardous areas of the building and who may enter them Staff should have, if feasible, changing and showering facilities, particularly following surgical procedures.
Staff personal items should be able to be held on site without fear of theft - and a policy requiring that facility to have been used before any insurance claim for loss is entertained is similarly important.
