THE RIVERSIDE PRACTICE
PRESCRIPTION RECORDING PROCEDURE

Introduction

The purpose of this procedure is to set out the method of issuing both initial and repeat prescriptions, and to specify the method by which prescriptions are recorded within the practice.

Computerised Prescriptions

It is practice policy that, so far as possible, all prescriptions are issued and printed electronically. This ensures that the medication history in the patient's electronic record is always up to date and available to all clinicians in the practice involved in their care, and that those clinicians are alerted by the system to adverse reactions and contra-indications.

All prescribing is using the EMIS electronic system which will store all the prescription details. This includes for home visits.

Administration staff are responsible for producing printed patient summaries [or downloading patient details] for clinicians to take with them on home visits. These details must include adverse reactions to medication.

In general, the following procedure will take place:

All prescription recording is entered in the patient record on the clinical system.
When a prescription is issued in the surgery it is recorded on the clinical system in the medical record under medications.
The record will state the prescription details and the date of issue.
The issuer will enter each drug as current, acute or repeat and will provide an indicator for the drug within the clinical system. The opportunity will also generally be taken to ensure that an indicator exists for all repeat drugs on the patient record.
When the prescription is no longer current, it will be filed under past drugs.

Nurse-Initiated Prescriptions

When a prescription is initiated by a nurse eg for travel vaccines, it is entered in the patient record and sent to the doctor for signing electronically.
The prescription is then checked and signed by the GP.

Nurse Practitioner (NP) Prescribing

Where initiated by the Nurse Practitioner, enter into the clinical record as above with issue recorded.
Non NP items as per nurses.

Manual Prescriptions

The practice does not use hand written prescriptions and all prescribing is using the EMIS system and electronic. Nearly all prescriptions are prescribed electronically using EPS
In the rare case that a prescription cannot be sent via EPS the duty doctor will access a blank electronic prescription from the store which has been logged to identify its serial number. This is then issued.
A private prescription for malaria tablets is created using the blank right hand side of a prescription paper.

Electronic Prescription Service (EPS)

The Electronic Prescription Service (EPS) is a service that offers patients the chance to change how their GP sends prescriptions to the place they choose to collect their medicines and appliances from. This means patients will not have to travel to their GP practice to pick up a paper prescription, but rather the GP will send it electronically to a place of the patient's nomination.

This can be a pharmacy or, depending on use and eligibility, a dispensing appliance contractor or a dispensing GP practice. The prescriptions will also be sent automatically to the Prescriptions Pricing Authority (PPA). Patients can change or cancel the nomination by notifying their GP, but should take care to do so before their next prescription is due.
