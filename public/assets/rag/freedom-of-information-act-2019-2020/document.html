Data Protection Update: 04/2020 Number 10

Freedom of Information Requests

Background

Freedom of Information (FOI) requests arise from the Freedom of Information Act 2000. These requests are different from Subject Access Requests that arise from the General Data Protection Regulation.

It is probable that practices may experience rising numbers of these and need to work out a protocol as a result. The good news is that you can treat these in a similar fashion to a Subject Access Request and log them in the same way. The bad news is that some of the requirements are different.

Under the FOI ,the definition of a public authority includes GP Practices but the only requests that require a response from you are those that cover the work you do for the NHS. Requests relating to private work the practice undertakes as a business are NOT subject to FOI requests and can be refused.

What is a Freedom of Information request?

Any member of the public can make a request for information of an authority receiving public funding.

A public authority is under a duty to inform the person making the request:

  • Whether it holds the information as set out in the request (this applies to information in both electronic and paper forms) and if it does;
  • To communicate the information requested.

You are not compelled to create NEW information: for instance to undertake a requested new analysis of your data. However, if the analysis has already been done and exists then you must release it.

Information you normally give out anyway, such as on your policies and procedures, does not come under the Act. Requests concerning environmental information are covered by the Environmental Information Regulations 2004 but should be treated along the lines of the guidance issued here, except that you have only 20 working days to respond. Personal information concerning the person making the request comes under a Subject Access Request.

Dealing with the request

A valid request under the FOI must be in writing not verbally (unlike the GDPR) and clearly state the name and address (either postal or e-mail) and will clearly outline what is being asked for. If the request is unclear you must ask the requester for clarification if, no response is then received after 2 months, you can mark the request as closed.

If the request meets these tests then the obligation upon the practice is to search for the information “in a reasonable and intelligent way based upon an understanding of how the (practice) manages its records”. If a search conducted in this way does not reveal the information, then you may consider that, “on the balance of probabilities”, the practice does not hold it. If you believe that a different authority holds the information sought then you should say so and provide the contact details.

However, the written request from a member of the public does not need to mention the FOI Act so in practice it is necessary to treat any written request as though it was made under the Act

Normally, you have 20 working days in which to complete the request. The count begins on the day you receive the request.

Please be aware that under section 77 of the FOI Act it is a criminal offence to “alter, block, erase, destroy or conceal” any information subject to the request.

Interestingly please be aware that if the Practice maintains a public social media presence such as a Facebook account, a valid FOI request can be made via Facebook and responded to in the same way.

Advice and Assistance

Section 16 of the Act places a duty upon public authorities to provide “reasonable advice and assistance” to applicants requesting information. This duty is enforceable by the Information Commissioner’s Office. What does this mean?

  • You should have a postal information and e-mail address where requests can be sent. (In practice add this to your website and noticeboard)
  • If an individual, because of disability or language, cannot make a request in writing the practice should take a view whether they are prepared to accept a verbal request or to signpost the individual to Citizens’ Advice to help them frame their written request.

Ability to Charge

Unlike under the GDPR, you are able to charge for providing the information to an FOI request although many public authorities do not.

You can only charge for the production and release of the information itself and not for the act of searching for it. If you decide to charge then you must notify the requester as soon as possible within the 20 day period. It is reasonable to set a period of 3 months within which the fee is to be paid. The requester has the right to complain about the fee via your internal complaints procedure and to the Information Commissioner.

You may charge for:

  • The actual costs of producing the information, e.g redaction of personal information as well as printing and photocopying;
  • Transmission costs, e.g. postage, cost of memory stick etc.
  • Other costs associated with the applicant’s preferred method of receiving the information.

Please note that the ability to charge can be dictated by the terms of your GMS/PMS contract. Please check if in doubt.

There is an appropriate cost limit of course. The limit is calculated at £25 per hour for 18 hours or £450. If your estimate is that the costs of provision will be above this limit you can refuse to deal with the request. When assessing whether this limit will be breached, you will need to assess the effort required to locate and retrieve the information and extract the data from other documents and systems. This should be reached by means of a “reasonable assessment” and not a precise calculation.

One strange disconnection is that while you are under a GDPR derived duty not to reveal the data of specific third parties in an FOI request, you cannot include the time taken for such a reduction in your assessment; however if the “burden (of multiple redactions) is unreasonable “ you can refuse the request altogether under section 14 below.

If it is assessed that more than 18 hours work will be required, the request can be refused. (Section 12 of the Act) but the refusal may also include suggesting how the scope of the request could be restricted in order to come within scope.

The details of every request you refuse as vexatious, together with the dates of request received and notice given should be included as part of your FOI/SAR log.

Hard to Resolve Requests

Some requests touch on information that relates to other, specified people (data subjects under the GDPR) or may even affect their interests. Although the boundaries are not clear, it is probable that once a different, specific individual is named, the GDPR will take precedence. If in doubt, consult your Data Protection Officer.

Occasionally, the request may include contract or other information that is the preserve of other organisations. It is wise to consult these third parties and ask them whether you can release that data or whether they would like to take over the response.

Any request asking “Why?” does not fall within the parameters of the FOI and does not require an answer if it would mean creating new data, unless you wish to provide an explanation.

If a request requires extensive information or you need to consult other organisations or individuals you will qualify for a “public interest test extension until such time as is reasonable in the circumstances”. Best practice says that this amounts to a further 20 working days and that you should inform the requester.

Disputes

Complaints about the handling of FOI requests should be subject to the practice’s normal complaints process, but a dispute unresolved by this process is likely to lead to a full internal review of the decisions around the information released or non-release of information. Such an internal review should be carried out by someone unconnected with the original decisions. Contact your DPO if you cannot do this otherwise internally. The requester must also be informed of their right to complain to the Information Commissioner if they are dissatisfied.

Vexatious Requests

As so often, the definition of what exactly is a vexatious request which can be refused is very vague. Guidance from the ICO gives some indicators:

  • An abusive or offensive request is clearly vexatious
  • The burden it places upon the organisation is unreasonable
  • Questionable motives lie behind the request
  • The potential value of the information requested – something like how many tea-bags do the nurses use would be an example or worthless information
  • Harassing the organisation with continual requests.
  • Has the requester made a number of other FOI requests.
  • Previous dealings with the requester (i.e is there history of a difficult relationship that could indicate a grudge or desire to cause trouble for the organisation.

If any of these conditions apply, the request can be refused under Section 14 of the Act, but organisations should always seek to refuse under section 12 first. Repeated vexatious requests do not require more than the original refusal notice to be issued.

The details of every request you regard as vexatious and why, together with the dates of request received and notice given should be included as part of your FOI/SAR log.

Publication Schemes

Section 19 of the FOI Act requires all public authorities to adopt and maintain a publication scheme. An example scheme is attached at Annex A for you to use on your website and in paper format.

FOI Compliance Statistics

Public authorities with over 100 (FTE) employees are encouraged to publish statistics on their performance in handling FOI requests. This is unlikely to apply to most GP surgeries, but you will need to keep a log for inspection purposes.

GP Pay & Benefits

It is a contractual requirement to publish these on your website annually already.

Hospitality and register of gifts

You should already be keeping a separate register of these.

Third party Contracts

If the practice sub-contracts to a third party a service for patients, as a public authority the practice remains responsible for answering FOI requests, but you should specify in the agreement that the contractor is responsible for providing information in response to FOI requests regarding that service. Contractors receiving FOI requests should pass them on to the practice to formulate the response. There is a joint responsibility to inform the public:

    • how the contractor can be approached;
    • the time limits that apply;
    • how any disagreement concerning the provision of information in response to a FOI request can be addressed with contractor;
    • how a request for an internal review by the Practice can be handled
    • the contractor’ responsibility for maintaining adequate systems for record keeping in relation to the service.

These considerations should be contained within the contract or as part of a separate Memorandum of Understanding.

Confidentiality clauses can conflict with your obligations under the Act and your broader transparency obligations to make available details of spending, tenders and contracts. Under common law a breach of a duty of (commercial) confidentiality is not enforceable in the courts where an overriding public interests justifies the breach.

Questions regarding the management of the service are likely to remain the responsibility of the practice but the contractor’s agreement to disclosure should be sought.

How to make a Freedom of Information request to The Riverside Practice

The Freedom of Information Act 2000 provides a right of access to a wide range of information held by public authorities, including the NHS and GP practices.  The purpose is to promote greater openness and accountability about what we do.

Our duty to you

The Freedom of Information (FOI) Act 2000 requires us to:

  • Provide information to you about our practice through a publication scheme
  • Provide a guide to this information
  • Respond appropriately to requests for information

Before you request information from us.

Check whether the information you seek is already available on our website. NHS England publishes information on its website also and you may well find the answer to your question is already there.

Our FOI publication scheme below sets out what information we currently have available or expect to release.

If you request information from us that is published elsewhere, we will refer you to the published source.

Who can request information?

Anyone, anywhere in the world, can make a FOI request of this practice

What can I request?

You can seek any recorded information that you think the Riverside Practice may hold.

Freedom of Information publication scheme

This section explains what information is routinely available and how it can be accessed.

The Publication Scheme is designed to signpost individuals to information we proactively release as and when it becomes available. The Publication Scheme contains seven classes of information, as follows, and information falling into each of these classes is published on our website:

  • Who we are and what we do
  • Financial information including GP earnings
  • What are our service priorities and information as to how we are doing
  • How decisions are made in conjunction with our practice policy
  • Our policies and procedures
  • Statistical data including patient list size
  • The services we offer

At present, all the information we release proactively is available free of charge on our website. If in the future, we make information available but do not publish it on our website we will update this publication scheme to explain how you can access it. We will also explain any charges that may be necessary.

These charges will be justified and transparent and kept to a minimum. Charges may be made for information subject to a charging regime specified by Parliament. Charges may be made for actual disbursements incurred such as:

  • photocopying
  • postage and packaging
  • the costs directly incurred as a result of locating viewing and collating information

Charges may also be made for information provided under this scheme where they are legally authorised, they are in all the circumstances, including the general principles of the right of access to information held by public authorities, justified and are in accordance with a published schedule or schedules of fees which is readily available to the public.

Charges may also be made for making datasets (or parts of datasets) that are relevant copyright works available for re-use. These charges will be in accordance with either regulations made under section 11B of the Freedom of Information Act or other enactments. Information held by a public authority that is not published under this scheme can be requested in writing, when its provision will be considered in accordance with the provisions of the Freedom of Information Act

If the information is environmental, we will respond according to the Environmental Information Regulations (EIR)  2004. You do not have to know whether the information you want is covered by the EIR or the FOI Act. When you make a request, we will decide which law applies.

If the information relates to your own personal data,  then you should make a subject access request under the Data Protection Act  (DPA) 2018, and not under the FOI Act. See below for how to make DPA requests.

How do I request information?

Email requests should be sent to nelondonicb.theriversidepractice@nhs.net

What information must I include in my request?

The FOI Act requires certain information to be supplied before we can respond to your request:

  • your real name – we do not have to respond to requests submitted under a pseudonym;
  • your address (email addresses are acceptable);
  • a description of the information you wish to obtain; and
  • any preferences for the format in which you wish to receive the information e.g. electronic or hard copy. We will endeavour to meet your preferences but cannot guarantee that we will be able to.

What you do not need to do:

  • explicitly mention the FOI Act, although it will help to do so;
  • know whether the information is covered by the FOI Act or the EIR as we will decide this;
  • say why you want the information; or
  • specify particular documents. You have a right to information, however it is recorded.

How should I word my request?

Comprehensive guidance on submitting effective requests for information is available from the Information Commissioner’s Office. However, to frame an effective request for the information you need:

Do:

  • do clearly identify the information you want. Be clear about date ranges or timescales. If it is not clear what you are requesting, we may need to seek further clarification;
  • do be as specific as possible. If your request is too general, it may be refused on the grounds that replying would exceed the cost limit laid down in the Fees Regulations, which is equivalent to one person working for three and a half days. If this happens, we will ask you to re-submit a narrower, more specific request which could be met within the cost limits and give you advice and assistance to do so;
  • do ask questions such as “what” or “how much” as this is much more likely to result in a useful response;
  • do use straightforward, polite language.

Don’t:

  • don’t use open-ended questions such as “why”.  We do not have to answer your question if this would mean creating new information or giving an opinion or judgment that is not already recorded;
  • don’t base your request on assumptions or opinions;
  • don’t mix your request with complaints or comments.

What happens when my request is received?

The Riverside Practice has a legal obligation to reply to your FOI request and must do so within 20 working days of receipt. We will do one of the following:

  • supply you with the information you requested;
  • inform you that we don’t hold the information and, if we are able, advise you who does;
  • inform you that your request will exceed the cost limit specified in the Fees Regulations and invite you to submit a narrower request;
  • inform you that we hold the information requested but refuse to provide all or part of it and explain why, citing one or more of the exemptions from the FOI Act;
  • inform you that we are refusing your request on the basis it is repeated or vexatious; or
  • inform you that we need more time to consider the public interest test in relation to your request and let you know when to expect a further response. This should not be later than 40 working days after receipt of your request.

What can I do if I am unhappy with the reply I receive or the way my request was handled?

You can ask the Practice for an internal review of your FOI request or contact pour Data Protection Officer. When you write to us requesting an internal review, we will acknowledge your letter and tell you how long we think the review will take. We aim to complete internal reviews within 20 working days, although more cases that are complex may take longer. Where internal reviews go over 20 working days, we will keep you informed of progress.

If, after an internal review, you are still not satisfied you can then complain to the Information Commissioner (ICO).  Details of how to do this are available at the ICO website.

Details of how to complain further to the Information Commissioner will be included in our response to your internal review request.

How do I request information held about me?

This falls under the General Data Protection Regulation as incorporated in the Data Protection Act 2018. Please refer to separate guidance on the Practice website or contact reception.
