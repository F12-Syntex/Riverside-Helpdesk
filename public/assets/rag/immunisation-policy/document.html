Introduction

This document covers the following immunisations for communicable diseases that are most likely to be present in a practice environment.

  • Hepatitis B
  • Tuberculosis (TB)
  • Measles Mumps and Rubella
  • Chicken Pox – (Varicella)
  • Flu Vaccination
  • Diphtheria
  • Tetanus
  • Poliomyelitis (Polio)
  • Coronavirus (Covid)

The Practice has a responsibility to do all that is reasonably practicable to reduce the risk of infection to employees, patients, visitors, and healthcare workers. The duty of care extends towards patients which includes taking reasonable precautions to protect them from communicable diseases. This data should be kept in line with the practice employee privacy notice.

The policy sets out the responsibilities of the employee as well as the employer regarding screening and immunisation. Immunisation is important and must be carried out in order to:

  • Protect the individual and their family
  • Protect patients from exposure (especially vulnerable/immune-suppressed individuals)
  • Protect other healthcare staff from infection
  • Allow for efficient running of practice services without disruption.

The policy sets out duties and responsibilities of Practice Management, the Occupational Health Department (OHD) and the individual staff member, with regards to health screening and immunisation.

Practices must show that there is an effective staff immunisation protocol in place. It also details the vaccinations required for staff.  This policy relates to CQC Regulation 12 (Safe Care and Treatment).

Purpose

The purposes of this policy are to ensure that:

  • All staff who have or shall have direct contact with users of the service are protected in accordance with current national guidance and as advised by the OHD; and
  • To restrict any health care workers infected with infectious diseases within the workplace where their infection may pose a risk to users of service in their care or other employees.

Pre-employment screening

The process for pre-employment screening of infectious diseases and the required vaccination programme is described. In summary, all new employees should undergo a pre-employment health assessment that includes a review of immunisation requirements. The Practice COSHH risk assessment will indicate the highest exposure risks in the workplace and those employees considered at risk of exposure to pathogens should be offered the appropriate routine pre-exposure immunisation.

Healthcare workers from outside the UK applying for a position or a training place must have standard clearance for serious communicable diseases. It is recommended that both standard and additional checks for serious communicable diseases be done in their own country before they apply for employment in the UK and provide the results in their health declaration to ensure they are aware of their professional responsibilities relating to preventing exposure to serious communicable diseases.

The Practice should make clear to applicants that any offers of employment is conditional on evidence of satisfactory health check clearance.

Duties & Responsibilities

The Practice Manager or Line Managers are responsible for ensuring their employees comply with this policy. Practices must ensure that all staff receive immunisations that are appropriate for their role.

Where employees refuse to comply with the policy, Line Managers will determine what action is required, including the possibility of disciplinary action or even dismissal in the case of an unreasonable refusal to co-operate.  

Where an individual is not protected by immunisation the Practice Manager is responsible for deploying the employee in such a way as to minimise the risk to the employee and / or to users of service and other staff. A risk assessment on the employee and job role should be done.

Occupational Health Department

The Practice must demonstrate an effective staff immunisation programme is in place and available as well as an obligation to arrange for this service at no cost to its staff. The OHD is responsible for providing a clinical screening and immunisation service and providing Practice Management with information regarding the immunisation status of their employees. 

Existing Employees

Existing employees are expected to co-operate with this policy and to submit to screening and immunisation unless advised otherwise by the OHD.

New Employees

All staff new to the Practice must be referred to the OHD, which will be responsible for ensuring that appropriate pre-employment screening and immunisation procedures are followed in line with national guidance.

***Insert Name & Position of Person*** will be informed if employees do not attend for screening or immunisations – further details are available in the “Access to Occupational Health Protocol”, as an assessment form will need to be completed.

Recommendations for staff involved in direct patient care

All staff should be up to date with their routine immunisations, e.g.

  • Tetanus
  • Diphtheria
  • Polio
  • MMR (measles, mumps and rubella)

The MMR vaccine is especially important because of its ability to transmit measles or rubella infections to vulnerable groups. Healthcare workers should also have immunisation against measles and rubella in order to protect patients.

Practices must show satisfactory evidence of protection, including documentation of staff members receiving two doses of MMR or had a positive antibody tests for measles and rubella.

Immunisation

Immunisation to minimize the risk of cross infection within the workplace is required.

Hepatitis B

(Refer to “Hepatitis B Policy”)

All health care workers, including all Nursing, Clinical and Domestic Staff and Porters who may be exposed to blood or body fluids are strongly advised to be immunised against Hepatitis B, which can be undertaken by the OHC. This will be in line with the guidance issued by the Department of Health in the Green Book.

Immunisation is needed if they;

  • Have direct contact with patient’s blood or blood-stained body fluids, such as from sharps
  • are at direct risk of being injured by patients

Immunisation against Hepatitis B consists of three doses of vaccine at 0, 1 and 2 months. A single booster dose of vaccine at 5 years is recommended with no further booster required on a routine basis.

If the staff member cannot have the vaccination or declines immunisation, then a risk assessment of the staff member’s role and work area will be undertaken by ***Insert Name & Position of Person*** and the risks discussed with the individual.

Tuberculosis (TB)

All employees are required to be immunised or have immunity against tuberculosis (TB).

If the employee has no evidence of a scar, or BCG vaccination, then an appointment will need to be arranged with the Chest Clinic for a Mantoux Test, and if required, a BCG vaccination will be arranged.

The OHD will need to be advised of the result. A copy of their immunisation record will be forwarded to the OHD by ***Insert Name & Position of Person***.

If the staff member cannot have the vaccination or declines immunisation, then a risk assessment of the staff member’s role and work area will be needed and ***Insert Name & Position of Person*** will be informed of any restrictions that may be needed to be put into place.

Advice will be given to the individual regarding the signs and symptoms of TB by the person providing the immunisation.

Measles Mumps and Rubella

Documented evidence of an employee’s Measles, Mumps and Rubella antibody status or immunisation details is required.

Evidence of protection would include proof of receiving two doses of vaccines that cover Measles and Rubella (e.g. the MMR vaccine), or a positive antibody test for measles and rubella. This should be verified by a healthcare professional (preferably a GP). Unless there is a reliable history of immunisation, it should be assumed that an individual is not immunised, and the appropriate recommendations are followed.

If there is no documented evidence available then vaccination or a blood test will be undertaken.

If the staff member declines immunisation or blood test then ***Insert Name & Position of Person*** will need to be informed.

A risk assessment will need to be undertaken by ***Insert Name & Position of Person*** as to whether the staff member can work or continue to work in that area. The risk assessment will be dependent on where the employee actually works and whether patients and staff are being put at risk.

Chicken Pox – (Varicella)

Chicken pox is a common childhood illness in the UK. All employees that have direct patient contact will be asked if they have had Chicken Pox. Evidence of immunity to chicken pox would include;

  • History of chicken pox and shingles infection
  • Evidence of immunity by blood test
  • Vaccination against varicella (2 doses of vaccines 3 months apart)

For those employees who are unsure, blood testing will be undertaken. If after the blood test, chicken pox antibodies are negative, then immunisation will be recommended.

If the staff member declines immunisation or blood test then ***Insert Name & Position of Person***will need to be informed.

A risk assessment will need to be undertaken by ***Insert Name & Position of Person*** as to whether the staff member can work or continue to work in that area. The risk assessment will be dependent on where the employee actually works and whether patients and staff are being put at risk.

Flu Vaccination

All staff involved in the delivery of care and support to patients will be offered the annual flu vaccination at the commencement of the flu vaccination campaign and in line with Department of Health guidance.

The Department of Health recommends annual influenza vaccination of all clinical healthcare workers to protect them from contracting flu as a result of caring for patients as well as reducing the likelihood of illness and any related sickness absence from duties.

If the staff member declines immunisation or blood test then ***Insert Name & Position of Person*** will need to be informed.

(A copy can also be maintained within the Practice as long as confidentiality is maintained at all times.)

Diphtheria

The main vaccinations against diphtheria are usually completed in infancy along with the polio vaccinations. The majority of people will have been immunised in childhood and diphtheria remains rare in the UK, however it is still common in some parts of the world. Vaccination may be advisable if there is no evidence of it, and if employees are from (or have worked in) areas where diphtheria is prevalent.

Staff should be up to date with their immunisations – any staff that have not received a primary vaccination are required to have a full course. If a booster vaccine is given then they should attend 3 months later for repeat blood tests to confirm immunity and repeat the boosters at 10-year intervals.

Tetanus

Most UK citizens will have had a tetanus immunisation in childhood with a follow-up dose during their teens. Five doses of the vaccine in a lifetime are considered satisfactory long-term protection. Those staff who are in occupations which expose them to tetanus will be offered immunisations to complete the recommended course.

Poliomyelitis (Polio)

All staff should have received three doses of polio and two follow-up booster doses at 5 and 15 years following completion of primary course. Those who have not received this will be immunised with Tetanus/Diphtheria/ Inactivated Polio Vaccine (Td/IPV, sometimes referred to as DPT).

Coronavirus (COVID-19)

With the recent pandemic it is imperative that the surgery acts to minimise any risk to staff and others and where appropriate a vaccine is offered to all staff as well as a record being kept.

Where a staff member refuses to have such a vaccination then a reason and discussion should be had between the staff member and a clinical partner to ascertain the reason why.

After that, a decision must be made after taking a risk assessment as to how the staff member can be gainfully employed and with the minimum of risk.

It may be that this cannot happen, and that employment may need to be terminated after investigations have taken place and professional advice sought.

In the case of Lateral Flow tests available for staff to take, it is advisable to give each member off staff a box of tests to be done on set days at home before work ( twice a week, for example).  Results should be recorded by the practice.

These tests can give an indication of whether the staff member has the virus and if so, they should not come into work until a further test at a center or by post is taken.
