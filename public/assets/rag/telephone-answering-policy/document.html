THE RIVERSIDE PRACTICE
TELEPHONE SERVICE PROTOCOL

INTRODUCTION

The purpose of this protocol is to set out the basic standards of service which patients may expect when telephoning the surgery and to provide a Code of Conduct for staff so that our standards are both high and consistent.

Most people try to be pleasant and friendly, but with today's pressures and stresses it is easy to become upset, angry or complaining. The public will quickly react to impatience, arrogance, aggression or apathy. However, even the most difficult people soon respond to a pleasant manner, enthusiasm and a willingness to understand and help.

The receptionist is the first point of contact that the patient will have with the practice, whether on a face-to-face basis or at the end of the telephone. It is therefore important to make the correct impression on the caller by following the procedures below.

INCOMING CALLS

Answer the telephone promptly - to leave the telephone ringing gives the caller the impression of inefficiency.
Speak clearly.
Use the callers name where known and possible.
Announce your identity - answer the call with the greeting [HELLO RIVERSIDE PRACTICE, XXXX SPEAKING, HOW MAY I HELP YOU].
Listen to what the caller has to say.
Ask them to repeat their request if this is not clear
Be sympathetic and patient with the caller.
Place the caller on hold before discussing the call with other staff, or whilst "breaking off" to attend to a related matter during the call
Tell the caller what you are doing if you have to temporarily leave the call or transfer it.
Do not leave a call on hold for more than a short time without referring back to the caller with a suitable update
Do not transfer a call without announcing the name of the caller to the recipient.
Find out basic call details before transferring a call to prevent the caller having to repeat.
Always say goodbye.

MESSAGE TAKING

Also see Message Taking Protocol [*]

Never entrust messages to memory, enter all messages as a task and send using EMIS to the relevant person. Make sure urgent messages are passed on immediately. The telephone contact must be recorded in the clinical system UNDER TELEPHONE ENCOUNTER AS PLACE OF CONSULTATION..
The name of the caller should also be noted. A phone number for return calls should be noted if at all possible and when they are available
Doctors or other intended recipients of messages are to regularly check their EMIS tasks for any new messages.
All messages of a clinical nature or where advice or other health related patient contact has occurred are to be entered with details in the medical record. This may be edited later by the receiving clinician
All messages taken by the reception staff with information relevant to a particular patient should be logged onto the computer. For example an enquiry to the hospital about when a patient's next appointment is due should be logged on the computer.

MAKING TELEPHONE CALLS

Plan the call - have all relevant documents to hand.
Be clear to whom you wish to speak and what you want to say.
Keep calls as brief as possible.
Do not leave messages with third parties or on answering machines.

CONFIDENTIALITY AND THE TELEPHONE

The rules of strict confidentiality that apply in General Practice apply with equal force when using the telephone. Please remember the following:

Ensure other patients do not overhear your telephone conversations.
Be aware of the practice protocols for passing information to patients e.g. pregnancy test results, blood test results etc.
Remember all information is privileged and must not be divulged without the doctor's prior consent.

DEALING WITH AGGRESSION ON THE TELEPHONE

Most people are pleasant and friendly on the telephone but occasionally you will get a caller who is either aggressive or impatient. Follow the suggestions below to help cope with aggressive callers:

Do not react back; this will only make matters worse.
Listen to the caller, and try to find the reason for the aggression.
Be calm and treat the caller with warmth and consideration.
In all cases where you need to terminate a conversation due to extreme aggression advise the caller that you are terminating the conversation and complete a Significant Event Report. Advise the practice manager immediately.
Note the details of the call, including the date and time, for the retrieval of the telephone voice recording (if available).

DEALING WITH EMERGENCIES ON THE TELEPHONE

Keep calm and speak in a warm and friendly manner. Be reassuring. Involve a colleague.
Obtain vital information regarding the call as promptly as possible. You will need information about the patient such as name, address and telephone number and location if different, as well as an idea of the nature of the problem.
Pass the call to the Duty Doctor if available and add the patient's name to the duty doctor list for that doctor.
If the Duty Doctor is out take a message and ring their mobile.
Remember it may be appropriate to send for an emergency ambulance.
Let the patient know what you are doing.

PUTTING CALLS ON HOLD

When callers are put on hold ensure they are technically on hold and cannot hear your conversations. Treat all telephone receivers as "live" at all times.
Go back to the caller to give them an opportunity to ring back or leave a message.
If there is a long delay, ask the caller to ring back or ask if you can take a message.
